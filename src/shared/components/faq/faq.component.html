This is a very tricky question to answer specifically, so we’d have to work on averages. Bearing that in mind, the average 3-bedroom home in the UK would usually have 12 panels, an inverter and 2 batteries.

Solar panels don’t work on sunlight, they work on daylight. The stronger the light, the better the yield, but any amount of daylight will convert into electricity via the photovoltaic cells.

There are 3 different kinds of inverter usually associated with domestic solar installations – string inverters, inline inverters and hybrid inverters. Hybrid inverters are the most common though, as they are designed to convert DC to AC, AC to DC and high current DC to low current DC. Which is laymen’s term means a hybrid invert will covert the power from your panels into power you can use in the home, be able to feed power from both batteries and panels to the grid .... plus, a hybrid inverter will control the charging of your batteries.

Solar Panels have no moving parts, so they need very little maintenance. The silicon cells inside the panel will degrade over time, at a rate of 0.5% a year. Most panels will come with a 25-year warranty, meaning they will still be kicking out 80% of their yield after 2 and a half decades. In terms of day-to-day maintenance etc, panels will clean themselves when it rains or snows, but if you live near a bird sanctuary or have lots of squirrels in the garden, you may need to have them cleaned professionally a few times a year.

Your Solar Fast energy system will run perfectly well without batteries, generating electricity for your home all day. However, the panels will produce more electricity than you need so you have 2 choices: Sell it to the grid or store it to use during the evenings and night. We prefer the second one, which is why we will always recommend adding batteries to your set up.

The number of batteries you need will depend on how many panels you have and how much electricity your homes uses. Not much point having 10 batteries able to store up to 30kWh if your array only has a peak output of 3.6 kW as you’d never be able to fill them. That said, you can fill batteries from the grid too, so you could fill them as you sleep on a cheap rate. We would say that you should get as many as is practical, within reason, and the average power storage for a UK home is 7 to 8kWh.
